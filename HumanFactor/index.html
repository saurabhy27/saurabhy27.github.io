Introduction

An aviation accident, incident and serious incidents defined by the ICAO in the Convention on International Civil Aviation Annex 13 as an occurrence associated with the operation of an aircraft, which takes place from the time any person boards the aircraft with the intention of flight until all such persons have disembarked, and in which a) a person is fatally or seriously injured, b) the aircraft sustains significant damage or structural failure, or c) the aircraft goes missing or becomes completely inaccessible.[1]

Aviation accident cases can be extremely complex, A hull loss occurs if an aircraft is destroyed, damaged beyond repair, lost, or becomes completely inaccessible.[2] And Reparable-damage: Occurs if an aircraft is repairable.

In many aircraft accident inquiries, an intuitive distinction is made between “mechanical failure” and “human error.” While “failure” is a relatively neutral term, “error” suggests fault, which is a human context that implies blame. Mechanical failures can be described quite accurately (e.g., engine failure in hover, oil pump failure, tail rotor control failure, fuel starvation, etc.). This is not as difficult as it may first appear, and an understanding of the “mechanical” failures of the cognitive system during a rapidly unfolding emergency such as helicopter ditching should increase survival rates.

Accident Phase

This following map is used to know, in which phase aircraft pilots should be more focused on flying. We have used Take-Off, Initial-climb, En-route, Cruise, approach, Landing, Standing phases.

Take-Off- When the aircraft accelerates to a flying speed that is particular to every flight, it becomes airborne, and the landing gear is retracted.
Initial-Climb- The thrust is reduced to climb power. This phase includes gaining altitude and increasing the speed step by step.
En-route- The en-route phase of flight is defined as that segment of flight from the termination point of a departure procedure to the origination point of an arrival procedure.
Cruise- This is where the bulk of the flight is spent. Optimum cruise altitude depends upon the weight of the aircraft, and typically, an aircraft will ascend (climb) as the flight progresses, and fuel weight is burnt off.
Approach- At this time, the aircraft has to be configured for landing, and this is predominately predetermined at the flight planning stage.
Landing- This is the critical stage of flight where the aircraft slows to such an extent that it falls out of the sky just inches from the ground.
Standing- Aircraft are standing on the ground.

With the use of filters, we can see different results for different sectors,
In the Civil sector, the Take-Off phase results in the highest number of injuries.
In the Private sector, the Landing phase results in the highest number of deaths and injuries.

Conclusion

It is important to keep in mind that the dataset is gleaned from Aviation safety website and DGCA accident reports. Some accidents whose accident report is not completed maybe not included in this dataset. This report aims to know the safety of air aviation in a different sector. And we found that the civil aviation sector is safe and record 15 accidents in last nine years with no death which are decreasing over the years and most of the accident is due to Human error and Engine Faliae at Take-off phase. And in Military Aviation is not a safe sector and record seven accidents, this is less than civil aviation, but we have to know that many military accidents not recorded. Reson of these Military accidents is that the Indian Air Force is still using ancient aircraft even some aircraft's production stopped. In Private Aviation which include VVIP Chopper, Training and Testing and personal uses has record 13 accidents Most of the accident is due to human error at Landing Phase.
